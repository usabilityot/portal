About Me

I’ve spent most of my professional life learning how people make decisions and take action in the world around them. My passion is helping create a world where people are empowered to live their best and most purposeful lives. Whether the challenge is recovering from illness, navigating uncertainty, or making an important decision, I’ve always been fascinated by the relationship between people and the environments they inhabit.

My first career was as an Occupational Therapist. In that role, I focused primarily on the human side of the equation: helping people develop the skills, strategies, and confidence needed to participate in the activities that mattered most to them. I learned that success is rarely determined by an individual alone. It emerges from the interaction between a person, their goals, and the physical and social environments in which they live.

More than twenty years ago, I transitioned into User Experience Research. While the focus shifted from people to technology, the underlying question remained the same: how do we create environments that help people succeed? Instead of studying rehabilitation and daily activities, I began studying digital products, workflows, and information systems. The common thread between these two careers has been a deep interest in the point of interaction between humans and their environments.

Over the years, I’ve worked across industries including travel, healthcare, and security technology. I’ve been particularly drawn to products that support high-stakes decisions—situations where the consequences of error can be significant and where people must often act under conditions of uncertainty. These environments reveal both the strengths and limitations of human judgment, organizational processes, and technology. They also raise important questions about how we can design systems that help people make better decisions without taking away their agency.

This blog is an exploration of those questions. I’ll be drawing from my experiences in healthcare, technology, research, and my own journey as a patient living with heart disease. Along the way, I’ll explore topics such as decision-making, human cognition, system design, artificial intelligence, and the ways people and technologies shape one another over time. My hope is that these reflections will be useful to others who are interested in building tools, organizations, and systems that help people thrive.
